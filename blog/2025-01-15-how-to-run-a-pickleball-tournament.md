Running a pickleball tournament sounds complicated, but with the right setup it's one of the most rewarding things you can do for your local pickleball community. Whether you're organizing a round robin, a ladder bracket, or a full single-elimination event, here's what you need to know.

<!-- truncate -->

## Choose Your Format First

The format you choose shapes everything else — registration limits, time requirements, and how you handle byes. The three most common formats for local pickleball events:

**Round Robin** — Every team plays every other team. Great for smaller groups (8–16 players) where everyone wants maximum court time. Easy to manage, and players appreciate that one bad game doesn't knock them out.

**Single Elimination** — Classic bracket. Works well when you have a large field and limited court time. The downside: half the field goes home after the first round.

**Double Elimination** — The sweet spot for competitive events. Everyone gets at least two matches, and the best player usually wins. More complex to schedule but far more satisfying for participants.

## Set Your Skill Divisions Early

Nothing frustrates tournament players more than mismatched competition. Define your divisions clearly before registration opens — 3.0, 3.5, 4.0+, or however your community typically segments — and stick to them.

## Registration and Payment

Collect registration fees upfront. This drastically reduces no-shows and makes your turnout predictable. With DailyPB's automatic payment system, you can collect via Stripe checkout at signup — no chasing people down for Venmo the morning of the event.

Set a clear refund deadline (we recommend 48 hours before the event) and communicate it in your event description.

## Day-of Logistics

- **Arrive early** — 45 minutes minimum to set up nets, lay out courts, and handle last-minute questions
- **Have a check-in system** — a simple roster printout or your DailyPB attendee list works fine
- **Build in buffer time** — matches always run longer than you expect
- **Designate a referee or score reporter** if using DUPR — accuracy matters for ratings
